Brief Outlook

Understand our purpose to inform you about Māori history, culture, and significance in Aotearoa New Zealand.

History

The ancestors of Māori first arrived on canoes from Pacific islands before 1300 CE. They lived in small tribal groups that were affected by the arrival of Europeans from the early 1800s.

The Treaty of Waitangi signed in 1840 established British law and government. However, conflict continued in the 1840s and 1860s as Māori defended their lands and local authority. After the wars, Māori lost land through confiscation and sale.

Impact on Present day

In the 1970s and 1980s, groups protested about their rights to land, and helped promote the Māori language and culture. It featured famous and impactful events such as the creation of the Waitangi Tribunal in 1975 to look at claimed breaches of the Treaty, The Māori land march of 1975, and the occupation of Bastion Point in Auckland by protesters in 1977-78.

Disputes and protests are still prominent today as the New Zealand government are looking at reducing and reframing Treaty rights.

Significance

Māori culture is central to Aotearoa New Zealand's identity, reflecting deep connections to land, ancestry, and tradition. By exploring these cultural and historical foundations, we hope to allow for you to go out and discover the enriching history and culture yourself, fostering appreciation, respect, and preservation of Māori heritage.
